Projects Expanded

PSYKO is the solo project I’m currently working on. I’ve learnt a lot over the last few years, and I think I have a much better understanding of how to keep my scope down while still focusing on the core value. A solo project is great for this, and the core loop I’ve chosen seems to work well. Its short and tight and so delivers the value quickly. Additionally, I think the short loops are good for headsets, especially the Quest, where playtimes are often shorter in median than your PC play sessions.

NeverBound

NeverBound was my first commercial project. I had bought the Vive back in 2016, and had begun working on a prototype of a gravity shifting shooter idea I had.

Shipping for VR wasn’t easy, and is where I first got to develop my optimization chops. Gave me a much better understanding of the rendering pipelines. I had to write custom rendering code because unreal didn’t have it implemented yet to be fast on VR. I had to write custom collision code because Unreal’s wasn’t made for the vast number of movement and sweeps we were doing. It was quite the challenging project, and possibly a bit over-scoped if I’m being honest for my first project.

The reviews were overall quite positive, but it always suffered from a lack of content, somehting that people expressed often.

Flappy Flappy VR

My original hypothesis for this game was that it might work because of two things:

  • Flappy Bird is recognizable and has viral potential.
  • People will look ridiculous while playing.
  • The input is so simple that a small child can instantly learn.

Here’s a video of Disrupt, a popular youtuber, playing it for 24 hours straight. Also, he broke his tooth playing it. If that’s not success I don’t know what is.

Disrupt playing Flappy Flappy VR for 24 hours. https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BAaH--wIhPI&t=13s&ab_channel=Disrupt

The irony is that while NeverBound, my passion project turned professional, made significantly less money than Flappy did. Flappy began as a small weekend-long project. The scope grew as more gameplay and biomes were added. Localization was added after getting a few licensing deals in Korea and China. Still, it remained light and profitable.

Manufactory -> Military Robotics Program

Possibly the riskiest project. We pitched an idea to the CMF about a factory game that would be 10000x bigger than Factorio. We got the prototype funding we had wanted and began building out the different modules. We posited that with special compression algorithms we had come up with for the sequences of items on belts in a factory simulation, we could get acceleration and exponential speed-up in factory simulation.

Military Robotics Program

Military Robotics Program, the pivot, is an Factory Automation - Real-time strategy hybrid. The user designs individual robots by combining different parts. They build factories that produce these robots. These robots go off into enemy territory to combat, and must be appropriately supplied with resources.

Ultimately, while we did have a lot of this theoretically working, it put a lot of constraints on gameplay, and we felt that it would be too risky to continue to pursue, for the actual gameplay wouldn’t be fun, which is what matters. Pivots are also risky, and it didn’t get picked up.

McGill Micrscope AR/VR experience - McGill BioEngineering Department

This was a nice change of pace for games. The application has the user setting up a exact replica of a popular microscope known as the Olympus XYZ?

The user goes through several steps to setting up the micrscope, and then it sits in the augmented environment for the user to play with. They can take it apart, look at the specimen and see the anatomy of all the pieces.
